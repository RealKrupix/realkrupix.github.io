VULNERABILITY OF SELF IN SOCIAL INTERACTION
In this presentation, I will discuss the meaning and significance of "self" in social interaction. Taking Erving Goffman's writings as my point of departure, I will argue that self involves display of who the person is; such display is accomplished through action in interaction. Self is often dormant, in Goffman's terms "tacitly coded"; sometimes events in interaction destabilize it and what is understood as self becomes explicated. ((Continued)) Many scholars have suggested that self is vulnerable to rejection and negative evaluation. To explore such vulnerability, I will present video recorded data from ordinary conversation, psychotherapy, and psychiatric interviews. I will also discuss the experience of self in narcissistic persons, showing extracts from narrative interviews. In line with Garfinkel's notes on psychiatric conditions as sources of insight on seen but unnoticed interactional practices, I will argue that the study of narcissistic persons can cast light on the construction of self in social interaction. I will conclude my presentation with a conceptual model that seeks to integrate self-psychology and Goffmanian interactional understanding of the self.
